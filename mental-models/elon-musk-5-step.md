# Elon's 5-Step Process

## Type
Mental Model / Decision Framework

## Summary
A framework for thinking clearly about and solving any problem by rigorously questioning every assumption before delegating, simplifying, accelerating, automating, and iterating in that specific order. The order matters — skipping steps leads to optimizing things that shouldn't exist.

## The 5 Steps (in order)

1. **Question every requirement** — Every requirement is a lie dressed up as a law. Ask: "Why must this be this way?" Don't accept "because we've always done it" or "because I said so."
2. **Delete** — Remove everything that isn't essential. If you can't describe what a feature does in one sentence, it's probably bloat. The goal is to have the fewest features possible that still solve the core problem.
3. **Simplify** — What remains should be as straightforward as possible. Design for the lowest common denominator of understanding. If it requires a manual, it's too complex.
4. **Accelerate** — Only speed up what remains. Don't optimize something that shouldn't exist in the first place.
5. **Automate** — Only automate steps 1–4. Automation applied to a broken process just makes the failure faster and more expensive.

## Why This Works
Most organizations do these in reverse: they automate first, then accelerate, then try to simplify something too complex to fix, and they're blocked by requirements they never questioned. Elon forces you to question before touching anything else.

## Key Quotes from Elon (Isaacson Biography)

> "Requirements from smart people are the most dangerous because people are less likely to question them. Always do so, even if the requirement came from me. Then make the requirements less dumb."

> "You may have to add [parts or processes] back later. In fact, if you do not end up adding back at least 10% of them, then you didn't delete enough."

> "A common mistake is to simplify and optimize a part or a process that should not exist."

> "Every process can be speeded up. But only do this after you have followed the first three steps. In the Tesla factory, I mistakenly spent a lot of time accelerating processes that I later realized should have been deleted."

> "[Automate] comes last. The big mistake in [my factories] was that I began by trying to automate every step. We should have waited until all the requirements had been questioned, parts and processes deleted, and the bugs were shaken out."

## Ownership Rule
Every requirement must have a **name** attached — not a department. "The legal department" is not acceptable. You need the name of the actual person who made the requirement.

## Application
- When starting a new project: question before planning
- When inheriting a system: delete before improving
- When facing resistance: "which step are we skipping?" is the diagnostic question
- The deletion benchmark: if you don't end up adding back at least 10% of what you removed, you didn't delete enough
