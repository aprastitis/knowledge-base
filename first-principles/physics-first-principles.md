# First Principles Reasoning

## Type
First Principles

## Summary
The practice of reasoning from fundamental, indisputable truths rather than from analogy (other people's conclusions) or convention (how things have always been done). The goal is to reconstruct your understanding of a problem from the ground up, so you're not limited by assumptions others made that may no longer be true.

## How It Works

1. **Identify the current belief or assumption** — "We know X because..."
2. **Break it down** — Is this belief derived from first principles, or is it inherited?
3. **Find the fundamental truth** — What physics, math, or hard constraint makes this true?
4. **Rebuild from there** — If you remove the assumption, what's actually possible?

## Key Distinction: First Principles vs. Analogy

| Analogy Thinking | First Principles |
|-----------------|------------------|
| "We'll do it this way because it's industry standard" | "Why is it the industry standard? What fundamental truth makes it right?" |
| "That's impossible" | "What physics prevents it?" |
| "Nobody has ever done this" | "What's the law of nature that prevents it?" |

## Examples

- **Rocket costs**: Instead of accepting "space travel is expensive because it's always expensive," Musk asked "what are rockets made of? What's the raw material cost?" → SpaceX
- **Battery costs**: Instead of accepting "EV batteries cost $600/kWh," asked "what are the constituent materials and their market prices?" → $80/kWh realized
- **AI capability**: Instead of "AI can't do X," ask "what stops it?" and find the actual bottleneck

## Warning Signs You're Using Analogy

- "Everyone knows..." or "It's well-established that..."
- "That's not how it's done"
- "We tried that before" (without understanding why it failed)
- "The experts say..."
